Using the quantum effective action in the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism, we derive quantum cor-rections to the semiclassical Langevin dynamics of a dissipative system governed by a macroscopic degree of freedom. We discuss the connection with the Ehrenfest theorem and show that, in the low-temperature and weak-damping regime, quantum corrections are determined by the zero-point energy of fluctuations evaluated at the classical underdamped frequency, closely paralleling the conservative case. We apply these general results to the resistively and capacitively shunted super-conducting Josephson junction and to an elongated bosonic junction, where quantum corrections can reach the percent level under realistic conditions. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Superfluids states of matter are characterized by macroscopic quantum coherence. Some of their low-energy properties may thus be described at the mean-field level in terms of a complex-valued order parame-ter, a classical field that encodes the collective behavior of the underlying microscopic degrees of freedom and is governed by nonlinear field equations, such as the Gross-Pitaevskii and Ginzburg-Landau equations [1, 2]. In par-ticular, the Josephson effect in tunneling junctions [3–7] can be understood as the dynamics of the relative phase between the order parameters of two coupled superfluids, which constitutes a single macroscopic degree of freedom. The classical conservative dynamics for the phase, al-though adequate in many scenarios, falls short of cap-turing two key aspects. First, in several experimental realizations the actual dynamics is dissipative. In super-conducting junction circuits, dissipation arises from the presence of external resistors [5, 8, 9], while in bosonic junctions it originates from the intrinsic coupling between the Josephson mode and the phonon bath of the con-densate [10–12]. Second, the phase has a quantum na-ture that manifests in beyond-mean-field effects. In su-perconducting junctions, this was established in pioneer-ing experiments by Clarke et al. [13–18], which observed zero-point fluctuations, macroscopic quantum tunneling, and energy-level quantization of the phase. In bosonic junctions, where the phase is directly related to the con-densate fraction, its quantum nature is reflected in the interaction-induced quantum depletion [19, 20]. In recent works [20–22], we suggested that the quan-tum effective action, originally introduced to study quan-tum effects in relation to spontaneous symmetry break-ing [23–26], provides a transparent framework to incor-porate quantum corrections to the mean-field dynamics of the order parameter. Moreover, it offers a systematic interpretation of effective approaches such as the modi-fied Gross-Pitaevskii equation, which is often introduced heuristically in the spirit of density functional theory and has found numerous applications in recent years (see, e.g., Refs. [27–32]). At first order in ℏ, the quantum-corrected semiclassical dynamics may be derived analytically; ap-plied to Josephson junctions in the absence of dissipation, it was shown to systematically improve upon the mean field when compared with exact quantum results [19, 20]. In this work, we incorporate dissipation into the quan-tum effective action, thereby characterizing its interplay with quantum fluctuations of the system and addressing jointly both of the key aspects outlined above. To this end, we adopt the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism [33, 34], which provides a consistent action principle for describ-ing dissipative dynamics. In this framework, dissipation and stochastic noise are intrinsically linked through the fluctuation-dissipation theorem [35, 36], reflecting the physical fact that dissipation originates from the cou-pling to environmental degrees of freedom (assumed to be in equilibrium) which are themselves subject to quan-tum and thermal fluctuations; these fluctuations, in turn, induce a stochastic force for the system. Our aim is then to derive quantum corrections to the deterministic component of the dissipative dynamics, which arise from quantum fluctuations of the system’s collective degree of freedom. We will show that such corrections generally depend on ℏ, the damping coefficient γ, and the temper-ature T . In certain limiting regimes, they admit a simple physical interpretation in terms of the energy of quantum fluctuations evaluated at a characteristic frequency. The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we de-rive the one-loop Keldysh quantum effective action for a macroscopic degree of freedom, emphasizing the con-nection of this approach to the Ehrenfest theorem. We discuss the local potential approximation and, in the low-temperature and weak-damping regime, the second-order derivative expansion. Moreover, we extend the results to the case in which the conservative part of the action is formulated in phase space. In Sec. III, we apply the general results to the resistively and capacitively shunted junction (RCSJ) model for a superconducting circuit and to an elongated bosonic junction, which provide concrete realizations of the two types of action considered in the previous section. Finally, in Sec. IV we present the con-clusions. Appendices A, B, and C provide a detailed derivation of the Keldysh action for the elongated bosonic

> IV. CONCLUSIONS

In summary, we have applied the quantum effective action approach to the Schwinger-Keldysh formalism to derive quantum corrections to the semiclassical Langevin dynamics of a dissipative macroscopic quantum system. Since our framework is based on an effective action, it provides a general description that is largely indepen-dent of the microscopic details of either the system or the environment, making it applicable to a broad class of dissipative quantum systems. We have shown that, at one loop, quantum corrections are encoded in a non-local functional σ(¯ x), representing the variance of quan-tum fluctuations of the system and depending parametri-cally on the temperature T and the damping coefficient γ.This result is consistent with the Ehrenfest equation de-rived from the quantum Langevin equation. Within the local potential approximation and in the weak-damping regime, σ(¯ x) generates a correction to the system poten-tial given by the energy of quantum fluctuations eval-uated at the classical underdamped frequency. In the additional low-temperature limit, where the zero-point energy dominates, the derivative expansion of σ(¯ x) can be extended to higher order analogously to the conserva-tive case, yielding quantum corrections not only to the potential but also to the effective mass. We have consid-ered both situations in which the conservative dynamics is formulated in configuration space and in phase space, as naturally realized in the RCSJ model and in the elon-gated bosonic junction, respectively. For both examples, we have explicitly computed the quantum corrections to the oscillation frequency and to the decay rate of the superfluid-phase amplitude, showing that they can reach the percent level under realistic conditions. Several directions remain open for future investigation, particularly in connection with current experiments. In both examples considered here, we have restricted our 8analysis to the Josephson regime; extending the study to the fully nonlinear dynamics would be especially interest-ing, as corrections to the effective mass will play a cen-tral role. Thermal effects may be systematically incorpo-rated, at least within the local potential approximation, according to Eq. (27). On the technical side, extend-ing the derivative expansion beyond the low-temperature regime represents another important avenue for develop-ment. In addition, it would be natural to investigate the fully stochastic dynamics by including the effects of noise arising from bath fluctuations on top of the quantum-corrected deterministic evolution. For example, the ap-pearance of a term proportional to ˙ ¯x2 in the EoM (33), induced by quantum corrections to the mass, would intro-duce a nonlinear drift term in the corresponding Fokker-Planck equation, which we expect to substantially mod-ify both the dynamics and the stationary-state proper-ties. All these directions constitute promising extensions of the present work, for which the framework developed here provides the technical foundation.
